## SECTION 1 — PROJECT DNA

**What exactly are we building?**

A flare-forecasting system whose core operation is not "classify this curve" but "model the causal handoff between two specific instruments." Concretely: two 1D encoders read Aditya-L1's Hard X-ray (HEL1OS) and Soft X-ray (SoLEXS) streams; a cross-attention layer makes the Hard stream query the Soft stream's history; a Neupert-consistency loss forces the model's internal behavior to track the real physical relationship between the two, not just the final label. The output is multi-horizon forecasts (1h/6h/12h/24h) with calibrated uncertainty, plus two diagnostic byproducts — a Causal Transfer Energy (CTE) index that visualizes the energy handoff as it happens, and a Neupert Deviation Index (NDI) that flags flares whose physics doesn't match the textbook pattern.

**Why does it exist?**

Because Challenge 15 doesn't ask for "forecast flares from satellite data" — it specifically asks for forecasting/nowcasting using *combined* Soft and Hard X-ray data from Aditya-L1. Most flare-forecasting work in the literature (and most likely most competing submissions) uses photospheric magnetograms or treats multi-channel X-ray data as stacked input features for a generic classifier. NC²AN is structured so that the relationship between the two named instruments *is* the model, not an afterthought.

**Why should ISRO care?**

Three reasons, in order of weight: (1) it is a literal, architecture-level answer to the exact challenge wording, not a generic ML pipeline retrofitted onto Aditya-L1 data; (2) it demonstrates real satellite-data literacy — GTI filtering, fill-value handling, MET-to-UTC conversion — that signals the team has actually opened Aditya-L1 FITS files, which most CS-background entrants won't have done; (3) the NDI byproduct is a standing offer to solar physics, not just engineering — a system that flags non-Neupert-conformant flares is a candidate discovery tool ISRO scientists could use independent of the forecasting use case.

**Why should society care?**

Flares disrupt satellite electronics, HF radio communication, GPS accuracy, and increase radiation exposure for high-altitude aviation and astronauts. A forecasting system that comes with calibrated uncertainty and a physical explanation (not just a probability number) is the difference between an alert someone trusts enough to act on and an alert someone ignores after the third false positive.

## SECTION 2 — THE STORY

A solar flare begins with magnetic reconnection above the photosphere. Electrons get accelerated downward and slam into the chromosphere — that impact is what HEL1OS sees, in hard X-rays, almost instantly. The energy from that impact heats plasma, which evaporates upward and glows in soft X-rays — that's what SoLEXS sees, seconds to tens of seconds later. This lag, and the relationship between the rate of soft X-ray brightening and the hard X-ray intensity, is the Neupert effect — a real, decades-old piece of solar physics, not something invented for this project.

Almost every existing AI approach to flare forecasting throws this relationship away. It feeds a model a flux curve (or a stack of curves) and asks it to learn, from scratch, with painfully few flare examples, that "hard X-ray now" predicts "soft X-ray later." NC²AN doesn't make the model rediscover this — it builds the relationship into the architecture itself. Hard X-ray asks a question ("where did my energy go?"); Soft X-ray's history holds the answer; cross-attention computes the match; a physics loss term keeps the model honest about it.

The payoff isn't just a forecast — it's an explanation that points to a physical mechanism a solar physicist already trusts, at four operational horizons, with honest uncertainty bands instead of false confidence, and a built-in way to notice when a flare breaks the pattern entirely. That last piece is the quiet ambition underneath this project: most hackathon submissions try to predict something. This one also tries to notice something new.

Beyond the hackathon, the same instrument-native, physics-constrained approach generalizes — to CME risk, to satellite-shielding lead time, to a future multi-payload space-weather layer drawing on Aditya-L1's other instruments. That's the roadmap, deliberately kept out of the current build (see Section 6 and the SWIS-ASPEX note below) so the core idea stays sharp.

## SECTION 4 — WHY EXISTING METHODS FAIL

Brutally honest, no marketing.

**CNNs.** A convolutional filter bank is good at learning recurring local shapes — an impulsive spike, a characteristic rise-and-decay envelope — within a single channel or across stacked channels. What it cannot do natively is express "channel A's behavior at time *t* causes channel B's behavior at time *t+Δ*." If you stack Hard and Soft flux as two input channels and let a CNN mix them, any Neupert-like relationship has to be discovered implicitly by the filters, with no guarantee it will be, and with flares being rare events, there isn't much signal to discover it from. Oversampling flare windows is a workaround for class imbalance, not a fix for the model's lack of causal structure.

**LSTMs / GRUs.** Recurrent models compress history into a hidden state and answer "given recent trend, what's next." They have no built-in notion of *which* past instrument's behavior mattered or why — that has to emerge implicitly in gate dynamics, and it's invisible after training; you cannot point to a hidden state and say "there's the Neupert relationship." At 1Hz cadence, an hour-long window is 3,600 steps — a long way for a precise, second-scale lag signal to survive sequential compression without being smeared out.

**Generic Transformers (self-attention only).** Self-attention over a single concatenated stream answers "which of my own past moments matter to my future." That is a different question than the one the Neupert effect poses, which is fundamentally a two-stream causal question: does Hard's current state explain Soft's recent rate of change? A self-attention model *could* approximate this implicitly given enough data and enough luck in what the attention heads happen to learn — but it isn't architecturally privileged to, and its attention maps, however pretty, wouldn't have a clean physical referent to point to.

**The honest concession this section requires:** attention-based and Transformer-based flare forecasting is not new. Published work already applies attention mechanisms to flare prediction — overlaying attention maps on magnetograms for interpretability, building BiLSTM-Attention hybrids, and training full Transformer architectures on active-region time series, with attention shown to localize at physically meaningful features like sheared polarity-inversion lines. **"We used attention and nobody else did" is false, and saying it in front of this panel will cost more credibility than it gains.** What's different about NC²AN is narrower and more defensible: it is cross-modal (Hard↔Soft, not self-attention over one stream), it is built directly from Aditya-L1's own twin X-ray instruments rather than magnetogram proxies, and it is constrained by name to a specific physical law (Neupert) rather than left to discover *some* relationship on its own. That's the claim to make. Not "first attention model" — "first time cross-attention has been architecturally bound to the Neupert relationship using Aditya-L1's own paired X-ray instruments," as far as this board could verify.

## SECTION 5 — WHY NC²AN EXISTS

**Why Neupert physics naturally leads to cross-attention.**

State the Neupert effect precisely: hard X-ray emission (electron precipitation into the chromosphere) leads or coincides with a rise in soft X-ray emission, and the *rate of change* of soft X-ray flux tracks the hard X-ray light curve. Restated as a question-and-answer structure: Soft X-ray's current rate of change is *asking*, implicitly, "what hard X-ray history explains me?" That is exactly the shape of a query-key-value operation. Map it directly:

- **Query = Hard flux history** — "where does my energy show up downstream?"
- **Key = Soft flux history** — "here is when and how much I brightened."
- **Value = Soft flux content** — the actual signal that gets pulled forward once a match is found.

The attention output is a learned, differentiable, trainable version of the same lag-correlation a solar physicist would compute by hand when checking whether a flare obeys Neupert. This is not a metaphor bolted onto an existing architecture after the fact — the mapping is structurally exact, which is the strongest thing this project has going for it scientifically, and the thing every other section of this document should keep pointing back to.

**Why cross-attention fits the challenge statement specifically.**

The challenge does not say "predict flares from Aditya-L1 data" in the generic sense — it names two instruments and asks for their *combination*. Most competing submissions will, reasonably, treat that as "concatenate Hard and Soft as input channels into a generic model." NC²AN instead makes the *relationship between the two named streams* the literal center of the architecture. That is the difference between satisfying the letter of a brief and satisfying its spirit — and it's a difference a careful reader on the selection panel (the Payload Scientist persona, specifically) is positioned to notice and reward.

## SECTION 7 — JUDGE PSYCHOLOGY

**What will make them excited:**
- The NDI framing ("this can flag flares that break the textbook") — it reframes the project from "another ML pipeline" to "a tool that might surface real solar physics."
- Direct, visible alignment with the literal challenge wording — a judge who wrote or vetted that challenge statement will recognize their own brief reflected back precisely.
- The CTE heatmap, if it actually shows a visible diagonal/lag structure on real data — a single good visual can carry an entire review.
- Evidence of satellite-data literacy (GTI, fill-value handling) that most entrants in an AI/ML-skewed hackathon won't show.

**What will make them skeptical:**
- Any "first ever" or "nobody has done this" language — see Section 4. This is the fastest way to lose a technically literate reviewer's trust.
- A claim of buildability that doesn't visibly account for the real calendar (idea submission in days, shortlist mid-July, ~2.5–3 weeks of real build time before a 30-hour finale).
- Whether Aditya-L1 actually has enough flare events, especially M/X-class, to fine-tune on — this is a known, real risk and an obvious first question from the Payload Scientist persona.
- Whether the GOES→Aditya-L1 cross-calibration is validated against anything, or just asserted.

**What will make them confused:**
- Acronym density without a single constant visual anchor — NC²AN, CTE, NDI, HOPE, GTI, CUSUM, TSS, HSS is a lot to hold in working memory across a few minutes of slides. Pick one visual (the CTE heatmap) as the thread that ties the others back to it.
- Ambiguity about primary framing: is this a forecasting tool or a discovery tool? Both are good stories; presented simultaneously and given equal weight, they dilute each other. Forecasting is the primary frame (it answers the literal brief); discovery (NDI) is the most exciting supporting beat, not the headline.
- A 16-node architecture diagram with no visual hierarchy between "core" and "supporting" components.

**What will make them reject it:**
- A pitch that reads as generic deep learning with space vocabulary layered on top, rather than physics-grounded from the ground up.
- Visible internal inconsistency (e.g., a framework mismatch between documents) that signals the depth is performative rather than real.
- An inability to answer "why not just use a Transformer/CNN" crisply and immediately — this has to be internalized from Section 4, not just written down somewhere.
- No evidence the team has actually touched real Aditya-L1 data formats (FITS structure, GTI extensions) — this is the single fastest tell of a team that read about the mission versus a team that opened its data.

## SECTION 9 — MERMAID STRATEGY

**Where Mermaid should never appear: the judge-facing deck.** The dense, ✨-tagged, 16-node flowcharts in the existing engineering documents are excellent *because* they reward slow, careful reading — exactly the property that makes them wrong for a slide a judge will look at for ten to fifteen seconds. A Mermaid-rendered diagram also has a visual signature ("default flowchart-software look") that subtly signals "we didn't design this for you" — a small but real credibility cost in a presentation context.

**Where Mermaid belongs:**
- Internal engineering documentation — keep doing exactly what you're already doing in the merger audit and architecture-diagram files.
- A hidden Q&A/appendix slide, available if a judge explicitly asks "can you show me the full architecture" — this is the one judge-facing context where the dense version is an asset, not a liability.
- Future notebook headers and READMEs, where a reader has already opted into technical depth.

**Best visual format, slide by slide:**
- **Slide 5 (process flow):** a custom, hand-designed horizontal flow — six icon-led boxes, deliberately not flowchart-software-styled.
- **Slide 7 (architecture):** a custom layered/macro-block diagram, visually consistent with Slide 5, six blocks maximum, color-coded by function rather than by "what's new."
- **Slide 6 (wireframe):** not a diagram at all — a designed dashboard mockup or screenshot.
- **Slide 3 (USP):** a simple two-path comparison graphic.
- **Slide 4 (features):** an icon grid.
- **Slides 8–9:** logo rows and simple comparison bars.

**General principle:** Mermaid is a planning tool. Nothing a judge sees should look auto-generated, even though the underlying logic is identical to what's already fully documented in Mermaid elsewhere.

## SECTION 10 — FINAL RECOMMENDATION

**Selection Probability:** 70–80% to advance past the idea round, *conditional on* the curation discipline this document argues for throughout (six headline innovations, not sixteen; one primary frame — forecasting — with discovery as the supporting beat, not a co-equal one). Without that discipline, realistically 45–55%: the underlying idea doesn't weaken, but a judge skimming volume will read density as noise rather than depth.

**Strengths:**
- Direct, structural alignment with the literal challenge wording — not retrofitted, architecturally exact.
- Real physics grounding (Neupert effect, H/S ratio, TSS/HSS as the field's actual bias-free metrics) that survives a domain expert's scrutiny.
- NDI gives the project a "discovery tool" narrative most forecasting submissions won't have.
- GTI-awareness and satellite-data literacy are rare and cheap-to-show differentiators.

**Weaknesses:**
- Feasibility claims (as currently documented) don't yet visibly account for the real calendar — idea submission in days, shortlist mid-July, a true build window closer to 2.5–3 weeks than "2+ months."
- Novelty language risk: any surviving "first ever" framing is a self-inflicted wound against a panel that includes domain experts.
- A still-unresolved framework inconsistency across existing documents (PyTorch idiom vs. a Keras-exported model) — small to fix, costly if a technical reviewer notices it first.
- Sixteen merged architectural components are currently packaged for an engineer's review, not a judge's — a packaging risk, not a science risk, but a real one.

**Hidden risks:**
- Aditya-L1 flare statistics for M/X-class events may be thinner than the pre-training narrative assumes — this needs an honest, ready answer, not a hopeful one.
- GOES↔Aditya-L1 cross-calibration is asserted as a bridge, not yet validated against real overlap data.
- The CTE heatmap's persuasive power depends entirely on real attention weights showing a visible lag/diagonal pattern — if real data is noisier than the concept implies, the demo centerpiece could underwhelm rather than impress. Have a fallback explanation ready if the live pattern is messier than the mockup.
- Other strong teams targeting this same, explicitly-worded challenge may also converge on attention/Transformer approaches — the defensible differentiator is the physics constraint and explainability layer, not "we used attention," and that distinction needs to be drilled into every team member's answer to "what's novel here."

**Opportunities:**
- The NDI "discovers non-textbook flares" angle is strong enough to be worth a short write-up beyond the hackathon — a credibility multiplier independent of the competition outcome.
- The frugal-engineering story (Slide 9) is low-effort and most teams skip it — easy differentiation.
- The roadmap-only SWIS-ASPEX framing gives a clean second-act growth story without risking first-act scope creep.
- If Aditya-L1 data does prove sparse, the GOES-pretrain-then-fine-tune approach itself becomes a transferable, ISRO-relevant technique for *any* young payload with limited history — worth surfacing as a secondary contribution if the data-scarcity question comes up in Q&A.
